# Aevum — two chapters & the two modes

*Companion to the master plan. Covers the Edo Japan and Renaissance Italy chapters in full — puzzle beats, the exact Foley to record, and the lore cards — followed by the Calm and Sleep mode designs.*

---

## Chapter A — Edo Japan: "The Floating Hour"

**Feel.** Dusk and soft rain. A garden that keeps reflecting itself. Vermilion against teal water, paper against stone.
**Palette.** Peach-to-lavender dusk skies, vermilion torii, teal still water, warm cedar, paper cream.
**Dominant ASMR textures.** Rain on paper, water over stone, wood-on-wood, soft chimes.

**Mechanic spotlight — sliding shoji.** Paper screens that, when slid, physically *reorder* space. The room behind a screen isn't where it should be; sliding one panel swaps whole sections of the structure, like a sliding-tile puzzle made of architecture. Sliding is also the chapter's signature sound, so every move *is* an ASMR trigger.

### Puzzle beats

| Beat | What you do | Illusion at work | Signature calming sound |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1. The sliding veranda | Slide a paper screen aside; two broken walkways align through the empty frame | Impossible connection via screen alignment | Shoji slide *shhk*; wooden sandals; first soft rain |
| 2. The still-water garden | Calm the koi pond's ripples so the pagoda's *reflection* becomes solid enough to walk | Reflection-as-path / symmetry | Water trickle, koi plip, a single bamboo clack on a timer |
| 3. The moon gate | Rotate concentric stone rings until the path threads through and the circle "completes" | Rotational alignment + forced perspective | Deep stone grind, wind, a distant temple bell when it locks |
| 4. The paper house | Slide many screens; each swaps a whole room into a place it shouldn't be | Impossible space (the core mechanic, full strength) | Layered shoji slides, paper crinkle, rain hard on the roof, a kettle |
| 5. The torii ascent | Climb a tunnel of vermilion gates that loops impossibly upward; advance one gate per slow breath | Penrose ascent + the breathing-bridge | Sandals echoing in the tunnel, wind through the gates, a low drum |
| 6. The lantern release | Float paper lanterns onto the water in sequence; the reflection completes and the era settles | Reflection resolves; resolution beat | Lantern paper, water, distant koto & flute, one long temple bell |

### Foley to field-record (record real materials, not stock)
Shoji screen sliding (light and heavy); paper crinkle and tear-free flex; rain on paper/thatch in three intensities; water trickle, drip, and pour; koi surface splash; *shishi-odoshi* bamboo clack on stone; *furin* wind-bell (glass and iron); wooden *geta* sandals on stone and on wood; stone lantern scraping into place; tea kettle and tea pour; *koto* string pluck; *shakuhachi* breath-flute; deep temple *bonshō* bell with long decay; evening crickets; wind through bamboo.

### Lore cards ("hour-cards" — softly whispered, one tap to read)
- **Ukiyo-e** — "Pictures of the floating world." Edo's woodblock prints were team-made: an artist drew, a carver cut, a printer inked.
- **No nails** — Edo carpenters joined wood with interlocking cuts alone. A single room, walled in paper screens, could become many.
- **Borrowed scenery** — A garden practices *shakkei*: a far mountain or a neighbor's tree is composed into the view as if it belonged to you.
- **The deer-scarer** — The bamboo *shishi-odoshi* fills with water, tips, and clacks on stone. It is prized for marking silence, not breaking it.
- **Lanterns home** — At Obon, paper lanterns are floated downriver to guide ancestral spirits — a farewell made of light and water.
- **Summer bells** — *Furin* wind-bells hang from the eaves; their chime is said to make the heat itself feel cooler.
- **The way of tea** — *Chadō* turns hot water and a bowl into a meditation. *Ichi-go ichi-e:* one time, one meeting, never repeated.
- **The long calm** — Edo Japan held roughly 250 years of peace, and arts, gardens, and crafts flourished where war once had.

## Chapter B — Renaissance Italy: "The Vanishing Point"

**Feel.** Golden hour over a stone piazza. The world only makes sense from the right angle.
**Palette.** Warm Tuscan ochre and terracotta, cream stone, dusty blue sky, cypress sage, a glow at the vanishing point.
**Dominant ASMR textures.** Quill and chalk on paper, marble chisel taps, fountain water, page turns, wooden mechanisms.

**Mechanic spotlight — perspective itself.** This is the one era where the real historical breakthrough *is* the puzzle. Structures only resolve into a walkable whole from one specific viewpoint — the vanishing point. You move the viewpoint to "find the right angle," and scattered fragments snap into a continuous building. Linear perspective and anamorphosis are the toolkit.

### Puzzle beats

| Beat | What you do | Illusion at work | Signature calming sound |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1. The apprentice's easel | Rotate the viewpoint until a broken colonnade lines up at the vanishing point and becomes one arcade | Forced perspective (tutorial) | Charcoal sketching, easel creak, courtyard fountain |
| 2. The anamorphic floor | Find the one oblique angle where scattered floor fragments resolve into a real staircase | Anamorphosis (the Holbein trick) | Marble taps, piazza echo, pigeons, a far church bell |
| 3. The vanishing stair | Walk a grand staircase whose converging lines make top and bottom connect | Penrose ascent justified by one-point perspective | Stone footsteps, chisel taps echoing, fresco-brush hush |
| 4. The workshop | Assemble Leonardo's self-supporting bridge / geared lift; turn it to raise a platform | Mechanical logic; tactile geometry | Wooden gears creaking, rope-and-pulley, dividers ticking, quill |
| 5. The fresco wall | Trace the orthogonal guide-lines to the vanishing point; the drawn lines become real beams you walk | The painted illusion made solid | Brush on wet plaster, a chalk snap-line, water in pigment, far choir |
| 6. The dome | Ascend inside the dome; align the herringbone courses and the oculus to flood it with light | Convergence resolves; resolution beat | Vast stone reverb, one sustained choral note, wind at the oculus, bells |

### Foley to field-record
Quill scratch; charcoal and chalk on paper; a chalk snap-line; fresco brush on wet plaster; pigment ground in a mortar; marble chisel taps at several weights; wooden dividers/compass tick on parchment; heavy book page-turns; wooden gears creaking; rope-and-pulley strain; a courtyard fountain trickle; piazza ambience (flagstone footsteps, distant pigeons, low murmur); church bells of several sizes; a sustained organ/choral drone; a guttering candle; the deep reverb of a cathedral interior.

### Lore cards
- **The vanishing point** — Around 1420 Brunelleschi showed that parallel lines appear to meet at a single point. Painting suddenly learned depth.
- **Orthogonals** — The guide-lines racing toward the vanishing point. Align your world to them and flat stone gains a floor you can walk.
- **The hidden shape** — Some images resolve only from one sharp angle — most famously the stretched skull in Holbein's *The Ambassadors*.
- **Leonardo's bridge** — He sketched a bridge that holds itself together by friction and geometry alone: no nails, no rope.
- **The impossible dome** — Florence's cathedral dome rose without a full wooden frame, its bricks laid in a herringbone spiral that locked each ring as it went.
- **Painting into the wall** — *Buon fresco* is painted into wet plaster, so color becomes part of the wall as it dries. Fast work, no second chances.
- **The workshop** — Masters ran *botteghe* where apprentices ground pigment and learned by doing. Many famous names began grinding someone else's colors.
- **Ideas, multiplied** — The printing press spread thought faster than ever, while "humanism" looked back to Greece and Rome to imagine a new future.

## The two modes

The game has three states: **Journey** (the story — solving the eras), **Calm** (unwinding while awake), and **Sleep** (falling asleep). Calm and Sleep are not the same soundscape dimmed; they're built on opposite principles, and getting that distinction right is what makes them work.

> **The core principle.** Awake-and-soothed wants *discrete* sound — the tap, the clack, the pour. Those triggers are the whole appeal of ASMR, and you want to *cause* them. Falling-asleep wants the opposite: *continuous, low, predictable* sound, because a sharp transient that delights an awake listener will jolt a drowsy one awake. So **Calm mode is trigger-rich and interactive; Sleep mode is bed-rich and hands-free.**

### Calm mode — "stay a while"
A free-play diorama of any era you've unlocked. No puzzles, no goals, no timer.

- **Touch is the whole game.** Every object answers with its signature sound, a soft haptic, and a tiny animation. Trip the *shishi-odoshi* and it clacks; ring the *furin*; float a lantern. In Renaissance: run the fountain, snap a chalk-line, turn a gear, ring the campanile.
- **It composes itself.** A generative ambient music layer grows as you interact, so the longer you stay, the fuller and calmer it gets — you're quietly making the music by fidgeting.
- **A weather/time dial** re-skins each scene and its soundscape: Edo as a rainy evening, a snowy dawn, a summer dusk; Renaissance from morning light to deep golden hour.
- **Optional breathing guide** at the screen's edge for anyone who wants to pace down.
- **Save a favorite scene**; export a short ambient clip to share.
- **Session shape.** 5–20 minutes of unwinding, fidgeting, or running it as a calm background while you work.

### Sleep mode — "fall asleep to an hour"
A scene designed to be left running as you drift off. Hands-free by default.

- **Setup, then let go.** Pick an era, a timer (15 / 30 / 45 / 60 minutes or off), and either "dim to black" or "audio-only" (screen off, pure sound).
- **Everything slows and thins over time.** The palette darkens (golden hour → deep indigo → black), motion slows, and the soundscape sheds its sharp triggers, leaving a continuous bed: Edo becomes steady rain on a paper roof with a far temple bell at long intervals; Renaissance becomes a fountain under a distant choral drone, a candle winking out. Reverb lengthens; tempo drops.
- **One soft "good night,"** optionally whispered, then the voice is gone.
- **No interaction required, no surprises.** A single tap fades gently to pause/wake — no bright UI, no notifications, no autoplay-next fanfare.
- **Built for the dark.** Mostly-black frames are kind to OLED battery and burn-in; audio-only drops the screen entirely. Volume auto-tapers near the timer. Every per-trigger mute carries over (misophonia care). An optional "sunrise" wake — light fading up with birdsong — can serve as a gentle alarm.

### At a glance

| | Calm mode | Sleep mode |
|---|---|---|
| Goal | Unwind, fidget, focus | Fall asleep |
| Interaction | High — touch everything | None required |
| Sound design | Discrete ASMR triggers | Continuous low bed, triggers removed |
| Motion | Lively, responsive | Slows toward stillness |
| Light | Full, adjustable | Dims to black |
| Music | Generative, builds with you | Thins, lengthens, fades |
| Exit | Whenever | Timer, or one tap |
| Length | 5–20 min | 15–60+ min |

### How the two chapters feed the modes
- **Edo, Calm:** a rainy-evening garden — trip the deer-scarer, ring the wind-bell, send a lantern across the reflection.
- **Edo, Sleep:** rain on the paper roof as the bed, a temple bell every few minutes, the last lantern dimming on black water.
- **Renaissance, Calm:** a golden-hour piazza — the fountain runs, chalk-lines snap, gears turn, a far campanile answers.
- **Renaissance, Sleep:** fountain trickle and a faraway choir drone beneath a darkening dome, candlelight going out one flame at a time.
